# 🚧 Project Friction

> **Understanding the hidden cost of success in open source.**

Project Friction is an independent engineering research initiative exploring why mature open-source ecosystems become increasingly difficult to coordinate as they grow.

---

## 🎯 Motivation

Successful open-source projects attract:

* More contributors,
* More users,
* More integrations,
* More supported environments,
* More feature requests,
* More governance requirements.

While growth is a sign of success, it also introduces new operational challenges.

Large projects often experience:

* Pull request backlogs,
* Issue overload,
* Contributor onboarding difficulties,
* Compatibility constraints,
* Documentation overhead,
* Maintainer burnout,
* Increasing coordination complexity.

Project Friction aims to understand the underlying mechanisms behind these challenges.

---

# 💡 Working Hypothesis

Our current hypothesis is:

> **As open-source ecosystems mature, growth creates scale pressure that increases coordination costs and operational complexity.**

In other words,

Large open-source projects eventually become more than software systems.

They become **socio-technical systems** that must coordinate:

* Code,
* Contributors,
* Governance,
* Documentation,
* Community,
* Releases,
* Compatibility,
* Long-term maintenance.

---

# 🔬 Research Questions

Project Friction currently investigates three primary questions.

### RQ1

How does scale pressure emerge in mature open-source ecosystems?

### RQ2

Which coordination activities contribute most to operational overhead?

### RQ3

Which coordination bottlenecks could be safely assisted or improved without disrupting project governance?

# 🧠 Research Philosophy

Project Friction follows an evidence-first approach.

We believe that good engineering starts with understanding problems rather than immediately building solutions.

Our process is simple:

```
Observe
    ↓
Collect Evidence
    ↓
Identify Patterns
    ↓
Develop Hypotheses
    ↓
Challenge Hypotheses
    ↓
Refine Understanding
    ↓
Identify Bottlenecks
    ↓
Design Interventions
```

The goal is to let solutions emerge naturally from research.

---

# 📌 Guiding Principles

## Evidence before assumptions.

Claims should be supported by observations.

---

## Problems before solutions.

Avoid building tools for problems that have not been validated.

---

## Wrong ideas are valuable.

Rejected hypotheses remain documented as part of the research process.

---

## Open source is human as well as technical.

Large ecosystems succeed because they coordinate people, not just code.

# 🚀 Long-Term Goal

The objective of Project Friction is not simply to study open source.

The long-term goal is to understand how growth creates coordination costs in complex software ecosystems and to identify high-leverage opportunities for improving their sustainability.

Any future engineering systems or tools should emerge from evidence gathered throughout this research.
